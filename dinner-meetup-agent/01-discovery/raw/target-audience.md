## Anti-Persona (Who Is NOT Our User)

**Corporate Entertainers:** 35-50 year-old managers organizing formal business dinners (商务宴请). Their needs involve hierarchy, private rooms, alcohol selection, seating arrangements, and face-giving rituals. This is a completely different product.

**Family Dinner Planners:** Parents coordinating multi-generational meals with elders and children. Their constraints are different: wheelchair accessibility, kids' menus, quiet environments, early dining times.

**Solo Diners:** People looking for personal restaurant recommendations. No group coordination component.

**Ultra-premium users:** People doing 500+ RMB/person fine dining. Their decision criteria are prestige-driven, not preference-matching.

**Non-smartphone users or digital novices:** The product is H5-based and assumes WeChat fluency.

## Social Dining Behavior

### Typical Group Dining Workflow Today

```
Phase 1: INITIATION (5-30 min)
  Someone sends "周末约饭吗?" (Dinner this weekend?) in a WeChat group
  → Chaos of gif stickers, "我都可以," "看大家," "你定"
  → Someone suggests a cuisine: "火锅?" "上次吃过了" "太热了"

Phase 2: NARROWING (30 min - 2 hours)
  Organizer searches Dianping/Meituan, screenshots 3-5 options
  → Sends to group, discussion fragments across multiple threads
  → 1-2 people don't respond (working, commuting, gym)
  → Organizer follows up privately: "看到群消息了吗?"

Phase 3: DECISION (15-30 min)
  Rough consensus emerges or organizer force-decides
  → Time negotiation: "周六中午可以吗?" "我周六要加班到12点"
  → Final restaurant picked, reservation made (or not)
  → Location shared via Amap link

Phase 4: LOGISTICS (10-20 min, ongoing)
  "谁先到拿号?" (Who arrives first to grab a number?)
  → Day-of: "我出门了" "堵车,晚10分钟" "到了吗?"
  → Bill payment: One person pays, others WeChat transfer
```

**Total coordination time: 1-3 hours across 1-5 days**, mostly carried by one person.

### Decision-Making Dynamics

**The "随便" Paradox:**
Chinese group dining exhibits a distinct pattern: everyone *claims* to have no preference ("随便/suibian/whatever") to appear agreeable and easygoing, but most people actually have strong latent preferences. This creates a social game where preferences are revealed indirectly — through subtle hints, private messages to the organizer, or post-decision complaints.

**Who decides:**
- If one person is clearly the organizer/host, they hold decision authority but seek visible consensus
- In equal-status friend groups, decisions drift until someone takes charge
- The organizer role often defaults to the most conscientious or extroverted person, creating an unfair recurring burden
- Decisions that are "proposed by one, accepted by silent majority" are the most common outcome

**Consensus mechanism:**
- Explicit consensus-seeking is performative — asking "大家觉得呢?" (What does everyone think?) is polite ritual
- Real decision-making happens through:
  1. Elimination of unacceptable options (too far, too expensive, dietary exclusion)
  2. Default to familiar/safe choices (火锅 hotpot is the ultimate fallback)
  3. Organizer's final call, framed as a suggestion

### Cultural Factors

**Face (面子/mianzi):**
Choosing a restaurant is a face-sensitive activity. A too-cheap place suggests stinginess; a too-expensive one suggests showing off. The organizer's reputation is subtly at stake. This explains why people default to familiar mid-range chains — they are "face-safe."

**Paying dynamics:**
- Among close friends: AA (split equally) via WeChat Pay is now the norm for under-35s
- Among acquaintances or mixed-status groups: One person pays (to show generosity), others transfer their share afterward
- Fighting over the bill (抢买单) is a recognizable social ritual among older generations, but fading among Gen Z
- WeChat group collection (群收款) has made splitting frictionless
- The person who organizes typically pays first and collects later

**Hierarchy:**
- If someone more senior (older sibling, manager, senior schoolmate) is present, they often get informal veto power or first preference
- Junior members rarely object to senior choices directly
- Age hierarchy influences but does not dominate among true peer groups (同学, 同龄同事)

**Dietary accommodation:**
- Explicitly stating dietary restrictions in a group is culturally tricky — it can be seen as fussy (事儿多)
- Common strategy: tell the organizer privately, let them manage it
- This is a key product insight: AI as a private preference channel

## Technology Stack

### Apps/Tools Currently Used for Social Coordination

| Tool | Role | Usage |
|------|------|-------|
| **WeChat** | Central hub | Group chat for all coordination, WeChat Pay for splitting, Location sharing, Group polls, Mini programs |
| **Dianping/Meituan** | Restaurant discovery | Search by cuisine/location/price, read reviews, check ratings, book tables |
| **Amap (高德) / Baidu Maps** | Location logistics | Check distance, travel time, share location pins |
| **Xiaohongshu (小红书)** | Inspiration | "探店" (restaurant exploration) notes, trending spots, photo-driven discovery |
| **Douyin (抖音)** | Inspiration | Short video restaurant reviews, food trends, viral spots |
| **WeChat Group Polls** | Voting | Simple multi-choice voting, but limited functionality |
| **WeChat Transfer/Red Packet** | Payment | Bill splitting after the meal |

### WeChat Dependency Level: CRITICAL

WeChat is not optional in the Chinese social coordination stack. Key facts:
- **93.5% of Chinese internet users** use WeChat
- **1.2 billion+ users** (2022)
- **450 million daily active mini program users** (2021)
- WeChat is described as "an almost indispensable part of life in China"
- Having a WeChat account banned "can cause a significant disruption to their life"

**Product implication:** The H5 app MUST be WeChat-shareable. It should ideally be a WeChat mini program for deeper integration. At minimum, it must have one-tap sharing to WeChat groups.

### H5 / Mini Program Familiarity: HIGH

- **580,000+ mini programs** on WeChat (2018 figure, likely much higher now)
- **450M DAU** for mini programs
- Users interact with mini programs daily: food ordering, bike sharing, utility payments, games
- The concept of "open a link, use an app, no install needed" is deeply familiar
- H5 pages shared in WeChat groups are a common pattern
- Users expect fast loading, WeChat login, and WeChat Pay integration

**Product implication:** An H5/mini-program approach is culturally native. No user education needed on the format itself.

### App Discovery Behavior

Chinese young professionals discover new tools through:
1. **WeChat Moments / Group shares** (primary) — friends share useful tools
2. **Xiaohongshu recommendations** — "必备APP" (must-have apps) listicles
3. **Douyin viral content** — short videos demonstrating tools
4. **Word of mouth in coworker/friend circles** — "你们用什么" (What do you all use?)

**Product implication:** Growth loops should center on WeChat sharing. A "share this plan to your group" button is not a feature — it is the primary distribution channel.

## Data Gaps

The following were not confirmed through accessible web data and represent assumptions or areas for primary research:

1. **Precise 20-35 urban professional population** — China's 2020 census age pyramid exists but detailed urban/rural + age + profession cross-tabs were not accessible. The ~325M figure for all 20-34 year-olds is too broad.

2. **Average group dining frequency** — Industry reports (Meituan annual report, Dianping white papers) would have this but were paywalled/unreachable. Estimated: 2-4 times/month for active social groups.

3. **Willingness to pay for coordination tools** — No accessible data on whether this demographic pays for utility apps. Assumption: free with premium features (more plans, customizations) is safer than upfront payment.

4. **Specific Shenzhen/Shanghai young professional income distribution** — City-level granular data exists (local statistical yearbooks) but not mineable. Shenzhen per capita GDP of ~195,000 RMB (2023) is an average, not young professional specific.

5. **AI tool trust for social decisions** — No direct survey data on "would you let AI choose your dinner venue?" Anecdotal evidence from Ernie Bot's 300M users suggests growing comfort with AI assistants, but social decision-making may have higher trust barriers.

6. **H5 vs Mini Program conversion rates** — Comparative funnel data was not found. Assumption: Mini program > H5 for retention, H5 is acceptable for first-time use.

7. **Cross-city variance in dining budgets** — Tier-1 (Beijing/Shanghai/Shenzhen) vs. new-tier-1 (Hangzhou/Chengdu/Wuhan) spending differences are significant but specific figures were not found.

## Key Psychographic Insights

### The "Effort-to-Credit" Ratio
The organizer wants to maximize *perceived effort* (looking like they put thought into it) while minimizing *actual effort*. An AI that does the work but makes the organizer look good has strong appeal.

### Preference Privacy
There is genuine demand for private preference expression. Saying "I don't eat spicy food" in a group of Sichuan-cuisine fans costs social capital. Telling an AI privately costs nothing.

### Decision Fatigue Transfer
Young professionals already make hundreds of micro-decisions at work. The "what to eat" problem is trivial but draining. Offloading it to AI is not laziness — it is mental resource preservation.

### Social Bonding Through Shared Quirks
The fun of group dining isn't just the food — it's discovering that three of you all hate cilantro, or that everyone secretly wanted Korean BBQ but no one said it. An AI that surfaces these "hidden consensuses" creates bonding moments.
